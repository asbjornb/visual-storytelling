The United States

A Story of Territorial Expansion

"...that these United Colonies are, and of right ought to be, free and independent States." Declaration of Independence, 1776
1783

Treaty of Paris

Birth of the United States

By winning the Revolutionary War, the United States secured recognition of its independence and gained control of land east of the Mississippi River. The Treaty of Paris doubled the area of the original 13 colonies.

"...that these United Colonies are, and of right ought to be, free and independent States." Declaration of Independence, 1776
1803

Louisiana Purchase

$15M price 828,000 sq mi

President Thomas Jefferson purchased the vast Louisiana Territory from France, nearly doubling the country's size. It stretched from the Mississippi River to the Rocky Mountains.

"The sale of Louisiana assures forever the power of the United States, and I have given England a rival who will sooner or later humble her pride." Napoléon Bonaparte, 1803
1818

Red River Basin

British Cession

The Convention of 1818 fixed the U.S.–Canada boundary at the 49th parallel from the Great Lakes to the Rockies. Britain ceded the Red River Basin south of that line—present-day northern Minnesota and North Dakota.

"...the line of 49 degrees shall be the boundary... as far west as the Stony Mountains." Convention of 1818
1819

Florida Acquisition

Adams–Onís Treaty

$5M in assumed claims

Spain ceded East and West Florida to the United States. The treaty also settled the western boundary of the Louisiana Purchase and saw Spain give up its claim to the Pacific Northwest.

"Spain must either control Florida or cede it to the United States." Secretary of State John Quincy Adams
1845

Annexation of Texas

389,000 sq mi

The independent Republic of Texas became the 28th state. Texas had won independence from Mexico in 1836, and its annexation extended the U.S. to the Rio Grande—setting the stage for war with Mexico.

"The Republic of Texas is no more." Texas President Anson Jones, 1846
1846

Oregon Treaty

The U.S. and Great Britain peacefully divided the Oregon Country at the 49th parallel, giving America a window on the Pacific Ocean. Expansionists had cried “54°40′ or Fight!”—but the compromise at 49° avoided war.

"The Country is ours. The tide of civilization must and will roll over it." Senator Thomas Hart Benton
1848

Mexican Cession

Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo

$15M price 525,000 sq mi

The Mexican–American War ended with Mexico ceding an enormous territory: all of present-day California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona and New Mexico, and parts of Colorado and Wyoming. America now spanned the continent.

"We must conquer Mexico... but not for territorial aggrandizement; no—for peace, and security." President James K. Polk, 1846
1853

Gadsden Purchase

$10M price 30,000 sq mi

A final adjustment to the continental border: a strip of land in southern Arizona and New Mexico, purchased from Mexico for a southern transcontinental railroad route.

"The territory which we acquire is scarcely habitable, but for the great object of a railroad to the Pacific, its value is inestimable." Senator Solomon W. Downs, 1854
1867

Alaska Purchase

$7.2M price 586,000 sq mi 2¢ per acre

Secretary of State William Seward bought Alaska from Russia. Critics called it “Seward's Folly”—a frozen wasteland. It would later prove rich in gold and oil.

"...a polar bear garden." Critics of the purchase, 1867
1898

Spanish–American War

Puerto Rico · Guam · Philippines

Victory over Spain brought overseas territories: Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. Hawaii was also annexed the same year. The United States became a global colonial power.

"It has been a splendid little war." Secretary of State John Hay, 1898
1899–1959

Pacific & Caribbean

American Samoa · US Virgin Islands · Northern Marianas

$25M for Virgin Islands

The early 20th century brought American Samoa (1900), the U.S. Virgin Islands purchased from Denmark during WWI (1917), and post-WWII trust territories in the Pacific. Alaska and Hawaii became the 49th and 50th states in 1959.

"I yield my authority… to avoid any collision of armed forces and perhaps the loss of life." Queen Lili‘uokalani, 1893
2025–26

Renewed “Manifest Destiny”?

Contemporary rhetoric — no actual territorial changes

In his second term, President Trump vowed to “take back the Panama Canal,” renewed interest in acquiring Greenland, and floated the idea of Canada as the 51st state. Denmark's Prime Minister retorted “Greenland is not for sale.”

"The United States will once again consider itself a growing nation… one that expands our territory… We will pursue our Manifest Destiny into the stars…." Donald Trump, 2025
