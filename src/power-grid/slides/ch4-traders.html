Enter the Traders

Add cable capacity between Norway and Germany. Even one trade can use it all

Energy trading is one way electricity systems stay coordinated. Markets reveal scarcity through prices: which plants should run, where power should flow, who carries risk. Hedging locks in prices ahead of time; arbitrage aligns them across regions.

In the visualisation, cheap Nordic hydro clears toward higher-priced Germany. The flow is physical and grid-constrained, but its direction comes from prices. Markets can misprice risk or amplify volatility, so when forecasts fail or shocks hit, operators step in. The system works because markets and operators each do what they’re best at.

Some trading benefits consumers; some is a wealth transfer to traders. Economists genuinely debate the proportion. ACER estimated ~€34 billion per year in consumer benefits from integrated European power markets—though one could argue markets can be integrated without being fully liberalised.
