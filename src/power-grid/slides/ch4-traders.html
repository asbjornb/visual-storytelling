Enter the Traders

Add cable capacity between Norway and Germany — even one trade can use it all

Energy trading is one way electricity systems stay coordinated. Markets handle most day-to-day decisions by revealing scarcity through prices (price discovery): which plants should run, where power should flow, and who carries risk. Hedging locks in prices ahead of time, while arbitrage aligns prices across regions.

In the visualisation, cheap Nordic hydro clears toward higher-priced Germany. The flow is physical and constrained by the grid but the direction and amount comes from prices. Trading does not replace system operators or regulation: markets can misprice risk or amplify volatility. Liquidity helps prices adjust, but when forecasts fail or shocks occur, operators step in to keep the system stable. The system works because markets and operators each do what they’re best at.

The honest answer: some trading benefits consumers, some is a wealth transfer to traders. Economists genuinely debate the proportion.
