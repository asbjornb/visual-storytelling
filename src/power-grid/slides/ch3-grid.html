Grid Pressures

The grid usually looks calm, but stress needs handling by operators and traders. Different stresses play out on different timescales.

Cold, Calm Week

A Dunkelflaute: a cold high-pressure system sits over Northern Europe. Wind output stays low for days. Demand rises with heating load. Day-ahead forecasts deteriorate, intraday markets reprice, and thermal plants fill the gap. Interconnectors to the Nordics saturate as cheaper hydro flows south.

Outcome
Peak price€150–200/MWh (day-ahead, Central Europe)
Duration3–7 days
What helpedGas generation, Nordic hydro imports, demand response
Cost impactWholesale costs rise; pass-through depends on retail contracts
LessonWeather regimes drive both supply and demand. Markets reprice over hours and days.

The Nordic Dry Spell

Low rainfall over weeks leaves Scandinavian reservoirs depleted. Norway conserves water, bidding hydro higher. Gas fills the gap across the continent. Forward markets shift as traders adjust hedging positions. If it persists long enough, political pressure builds.

Outcome
Peak price€130–230/MWh (sustained, varies by zone)
DurationWeeks to months
What helpedForward hedging, import rerouting, reservoir management
Cost impactSlow burn — costs spread across months of higher wholesale prices
LessonEnergy scarcity grinds. Markets adjust, but the timeline favours those who hedged early.

Reactor Trip

A large reactor (~1.3 GW) trips unexpectedly. Automatic reserves catch the frequency dip within seconds. Intraday markets reprice within minutes as neighbouring zones ramp up and interconnectors load. Prices spike locally, then partially converge as the system rebalances.

In 2022, stress corrosion sidelined much of France’s nuclear fleet for months — a far larger version of this. Forward contract prices briefly exceeded €1,000/MWh.

Outcome
Peak price€200–350/MWh (intraday, around France)
DurationHours (single trip); months if fleet-wide
What helpedAutomatic reserves, then intraday trading and cross-border flows
Cost impactIntraday spike; broader market impact depends on duration
LessonSudden loss is an operator problem. Sustained loss becomes a market and political problem.

Windy Spring Weekend

A deep low-pressure system drives strong winds across the North Sea on a mild spring weekend. Solar adds output during the day. With factories shut and heating off, supply overwhelms demand. Day-ahead prices in Germany and Denmark turn deeply negative — generators effectively pay to stay connected. Interconnectors max out pushing surplus to neighbours.

Why don’t turbines just switch off? Many wind farms receive feed-in premiums on top of the market price. A farm earning a €70/MWh subsidy still profits at −€60. Prices must go deeply negative before curtailment makes financial sense — which is why episodes last hours, not minutes.

Outcome
Trough price−€50 to −€80/MWh (day-ahead, Germany & Denmark)
Duration6–18 hours per episode; clusters on windy weekends
What helpedWind curtailment, interconnector exports, flexible demand (batteries, heat pumps)
Cost impactNegative revenue for unhedged generators; opportunity for flexible consumers
LessonAbundance has its own costs. The grid needs flexibility in both directions.

Illustrative prices based on real patterns, not historical data. See live cross-border flows on Electricity Maps.
